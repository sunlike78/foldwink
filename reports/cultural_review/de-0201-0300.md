### 1

- **File:** de-0204.json
- **Title:** "Deutsches Kaiserreich"
- **Group:** "Kaiser"
- **Item:** "Karl I."
- **Issue:** Karl I. was Emperor of Austria-Hungary (reigned 1916–1918), never Kaiser of the Deutsches Kaiserreich. The German Kaiserreich had only three Kaisers: Wilhelm I., Friedrich III., Wilhelm II. — all correctly listed alongside Karl I. here. His inclusion creates a direct factual error.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Karl I." with a fourth figure tied to the German Kaiserreich, e.g. "Friedrich I. (Barbarossa)" if extending the label to pre-modern emperors, or remove the item and restructure as three confirmed Kaisers plus one plausible false-trail figure from the Habsburg line clearly labeled as such.

### 2

- **File:** de-0228.json
- **Title:** "Nibelungensage"
- **Group:** "Schätze und Gegenstände"
- **Item:** "Gungnir"
- **Issue:** Gungnir is Odin's spear in Norse mythology (Prose Edda, Poetic Edda). It does not appear in the Nibelungenlied or the Nibelungensage tradition. A German-speaking player who knows the Nibelungen will immediately identify this as belonging to a different mythological cycle and feel cheated, because no Nibelungen weapon is named Gungnir. The puzzle has "Tarnkappe" and "Balmung" which are authentic; Gungnir is not.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Gungnir" with a genuine Nibelungen object, e.g. "Siegfrieds Lanze" or "Hagens Speer," or expand the label to "Germanische Schätze" and note the cross-tradition nature.

### 3

- **File:** de-0233.json
- **Title:** "Moderne Deutsche Literatur"
- **Group:** "Werke 20. Jh."
- **Item:** "Der Prozess"
- **Issue:** Der Prozess (The Trial) is by Franz Kafka, an Austrian Czech author (born Prague, 1883; wrote in German but was a Habsburg/Czechoslovak subject). The puzzle is titled "Moderne Deutsche Literatur" and the parallel group "Autoren 20. Jh." contains exclusively German nationals (Thomas Mann, Brecht, Böll, Grass). Listing a Kafka novel as an example of "deutsche Literatur" in this national framing conflates Austrian-Czech German-language literature with German national literature — a common but editorially sloppy error that may confuse or mislead players.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Der Prozess" with a work by a German author, e.g. "Jakob der Lügner" (Jurek Becker) or "Homo Faber" (Max Frisch is Swiss, so skip) — or best: "Im Westen nichts Neues" (Remarque). Alternatively rename the puzzle to "Deutschsprachige Literatur" to legitimise Kafka.

### 4

- **File:** de-0243.json
- **Title:** "Musik in Deutschland"
- **Group:** "Deutsche Pop-Künstler"
- **Item:** "Udo Jürgens"
- **Issue:** Udo Jürgens (born Udo Jürgen Bockelmann, Klagenfurt 1934, died 2014) was Austrian, not German. The group is explicitly labelled "Deutsche Pop-Künstler" in a puzzle titled "Musik in Deutschland." He had enormous success in the German-speaking world but calling him a German artist is factually wrong. Austrian players in particular will notice this immediately.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Udo Jürgens" with a genuinely German pop artist, e.g. "Udo Lindenberg" or "Peter Maffay" (who is already in the same group — Peter Maffay is Romanian-born but German national). If Udo Jürgens is retained, rename the group to "Deutschsprachige Pop-Künstler."

### 5

- **File:** de-0246.json
- **Title:** "Elektronische Musik"
- **Group:** "Berühmte Clubs"
- **Item:** "Fabric"
- **Issue:** Fabric is a London nightclub (Farringdon, London EC1), not a German venue. The other three clubs in the group — Berghain (Berlin), Watergate (Berlin), Robert Johnson (Offenbach) — are all German. The puzzle's entire context is German electronic music. A British club appearing without any framing is a geographic non-sequitur. The group label does not claim Germany-only, but the implied context makes Fabric jarring and potentially misleading.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Fabric" with a German electronic club, e.g. "Tresor" (Berlin) or "Tunnel" (Hamburg) or "Rote Sonne" (Munich).

### 6

- **File:** de-0252.json
- **Title:** "Bauhaus"
- **Group:** "Bauhaus-Standorte"
- **Item:** "Chicago"
- **Issue:** The Bauhaus as an institution operated at three locations: Weimar (1919–1925), Dessau (1925–1932), Berlin (1932–1933). It was dissolved by the Nazis in 1933. Chicago (specifically the New Bauhaus / School of Design, founded 1937 by László Moholy-Nagy) was a successor institution, not the Bauhaus itself. Listing Chicago as a "Bauhaus-Standort" is a factual error — the Bauhaus never had a campus in Chicago.
- **Suggested fix:** Remove "Chicago" and add the actual fourth Bauhaus city — noting that the Berlin school was a private institute. A better fourth item would be a Bauhaus-affiliated location within Germany, e.g. "Erfurt" (early exhibition ties) or keep three confirmed cities and rethink the group structure. Alternatively use "New Bauhaus" as a separate concept and label the group accordingly.

### 7

- **File:** de-0260.json
- **Title:** "Jugendstil"
- **Group:** "Jugendstil-Kunsthandwerk"
- **Item:** "Tiffany-Lampe"
- **Issue:** Louis Comfort Tiffany was American (New York, 1848–1933). While his leaded-glass work is contemporaneous with and aesthetically related to European Art Nouveau / Jugendstil, the "Tiffany lamp" is a specifically American product line, not a German/Austrian/Swiss Jugendstil artefact. The other group members (Zinnvase, Keramikfliesen, Emailschmuck) are generic Central European craft objects. Naming a branded American product creates a cultural mismatch; German players associate Jugendstil Kunsthandwerk with WMF, Zinn-König, or Meissen output, not Tiffany Studios New York.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Tiffany-Lampe" with a German or Austrian Jugendstil craft object, e.g. "WMF-Krug," "Meissener Jugendstilvase," or "Jugendstil-Brosche."

### 8

- **File:** de-0262.json
- **Title:** "Deutsche Modemarken"
- **Group:** "Streetwear-Marken"
- **Item:** "G-Star Raw"
- **Issue:** G-Star Raw was founded in Amsterdam, Netherlands (1989) and is a Dutch brand. The puzzle is titled "Deutsche Modemarken" and every other group contains genuinely German brands (Adidas, Puma, Hugo Boss, Birkenstock, etc.). G-Star Raw is not German and should not appear in a German fashion brands puzzle without qualification.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "G-Star Raw" with a German streetwear or casual brand, e.g. "Trigema," "Tom Tailor," "s.Oliver," or "Bench" (UK but widely perceived as German — also imperfect). Best replacement: "Trigema" (Burladingen, Baden-Württemberg) or "s.Oliver."
